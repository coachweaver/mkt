Required Sections

1. Purpose Statement

A clear, concise statement (2-4 sentences) explaining what your club does and why it should exist.

Answer these questions:

  • What is the club's main focus or activity?
  • What need or interest does it serve?
  • What value does it provide to members?
Example

The Creative Writing Club provides students with a supportive community to develop their writing skills across multiple genres. Members will workshop their creative pieces, participate in writing challenges, and prepare submissions for literary competitions. This club addresses the need for creative outlets and helps students build portfolios for college applications.

2. Meeting Schedule & Target Audience

Include:

  • Meeting Frequency: How often will the club meet?
  • Meeting Duration: How long are meetings?
  • Proposed Day/Time: When would meetings ideally occur?
  • Target Audience: Who is this club for?
Example

The Creative Writing Club will meet every Tuesday during lunch (30 minutes) in Room 204. Meetings are open to students in grades 9-12 of all skill levels, from beginners exploring writing for the first time to advanced writers preparing for college creative writing programs.

3. Required Resources & Leadership Structure

Include:

  • Space Needs: What type of room or space do you need?
  • Equipment/Supplies: List specific items needed (be realistic)
  • Budget: Approximate costs if applicable
  • Leadership Roles: What positions will the club have?
  • Faculty Advisor: Have you identified one, or are you seeking one?
Example

The club requires a classroom with moveable seating to facilitate small group workshops. Initial supplies include printer paper for manuscript copies and basic writing prompts (estimated $30/semester). Leadership will consist of a President, Vice President, and Workshop Coordinator elected by members. Ms. Johnson has expressed interest in serving as faculty advisor.

Writing Tips

Your proposal should be:

  • Professional: Use formal language, avoid slang
  • Specific: Provide concrete details, not vague statements
  • Realistic: Show you've thought through logistics
  • Concise: Every sentence should add value
  • Action-Oriented: Focus on what the club will DO

Brand Kit: Colors & Typography

Color Theory Basics

Colors evoke emotions and communicate messages before words do. When building your brand, think strategically about what feelings you want your club to convey.

Color Psychology

Red
Energy, passion, urgency, excitement
Teal/Cyan
Calm, trust, innovation, clarity
Yellow
Optimism, creativity, warmth, attention
Green
Growth, nature, balance, health
Purple
Creativity, luxury, imagination, wisdom
Pink
Compassion, playfulness, youth, care

Your Color Palette Requirements

  • 2-3 Primary Colors - Your main brand colors that appear most frequently
  • 2-3 Accent Colors - Supporting colors used sparingly for emphasis
Critical Rule

Always ensure high contrast between text and background. Dark text on light backgrounds OR light text on dark backgrounds. Never light on light or dark on dark.

Color Harmony Strategies

Strategy Description Example
Complementary Colors opposite on the color wheel Blue + Orange, Purple + Yellow
Analogous Colors next to each other on the wheel Blue + Blue-Green + Green
Monochromatic Different shades of the same color Light Pink + Rose + Deep Maroon
Triadic Three colors evenly spaced on the wheel Red + Yellow + Blue

Typography & Font Selection

The Four Font Categories

Category Characteristics Best For
Serif Decorative strokes at ends of letters Traditional, formal, trustworthy contexts
Sans-Serif Clean lines, no decorative strokes Modern, digital, body text
Display/Decorative Unique, stylized, eye-catching Titles and headlines ONLY
Script/Handwritten Mimics handwriting or calligraphy Accents and special emphasis

Your Font Requirements

  • 1 Heading/Title Font - Used for club name and major headings (bold, attention-grabbing)
  • 1 Body Font - Used for paragraphs and regular text (highly readable)
  • 1 Fun/Alternative Font - Used for special accents or creative elements (adds personality)
Pro Tip

Great branding uses contrast. If your heading font is bold and geometric, choose a softer body font. If your heading is elegant and serif, pick a clean sans-serif for body text.

Proven Font Combinations

Modern & Professional

Heading: Montserrat Bold (sans-serif)
Body: Open Sans (sans-serif)
Accent: Permanent Marker (display)

Classic & Trustworthy

Heading: Playfair Display (serif)
Body: Lato (sans-serif)
Accent: Great Vibes (script)

Bold & Energetic

Heading: Bebas Neue (display sans-serif)
Body: Roboto (sans-serif)
Accent: Anton (display)

DO

  • Choose colors that reflect your club's purpose
  • Test colors together before finalizing
  • Use primary colors most, accents sparingly
  • Pair fonts with contrasting styles
  • Use body font for 80% of text
  • Stick to your 3 chosen fonts consistently

DON'T

  • Use more than 6 total colors
  • Use neon colors as primary brand colors
  • Make every color equally prominent
  • Use more than 3 fonts in your design
  • Pair two decorative fonts together
  • Use script fonts for body paragraphs

Flyer Design Principles

Required Flyer Elements

All Elements Must Be Present

  • Brand fonts (heading, body, alternative) from your brand kit
  • Brand colors (primary and accent) from your brand kit
  • Club title/name - large and prominent
  • Purpose statement (value proposition)
  • Meeting date
  • Meeting time
  • Meeting location
  • Call to action (tells reader what to do next)
  • Creative eye-catching element that makes it unique

Advertising Principles

1. Visual Hierarchy

Guide the viewer's eye through your design in order of importance:

  • Primary: Club name and eye-catching visual (largest, most prominent)
  • Secondary: Purpose statement and meeting date/time (medium size)
  • Tertiary: Additional details and call to action (smallest, but still readable)

2. The "3-Second Rule"

A person should understand what your club is about within 3 seconds of seeing your flyer. Use large, clear headings and minimal text. If you need more than 15 words to explain your club, it's too complicated.

3. White Space is Your Friend

Don't fill every inch of the page. Empty space makes your design look professional and makes important information stand out. Cramped designs look amateur and are hard to read.

4. Contrast Creates Impact

High contrast between elements (large vs. small, dark vs. light, bold vs. thin) creates visual interest and draws attention.

5. Call to Action (CTA)

Tell people exactly what you want them to do. Examples:

  • "Scan to sign up!"
  • "See you there!"
  • "Join us Monday!"
  • "Follow @clubinstagram for updates"
  • "Text 'CHESS' to 12345"

Creative Eye-Catching Elements

Your flyer must include something unique that differentiates it. Consider:

Strategy Description
Unexpected Layout Diagonal text, circular arrangements, asymmetrical design
Bold Graphic Element Large icon, illustration, or photo that dominates
Interactive Element QR code, tear-off tabs, perforated RSVP
Typography as Art Creative letterforms, overlapping text, outlined letters
Texture or Pattern Unique background pattern that supports theme
Color Block Design Bold geometric color sections organizing information
Minimalist Approach Ultra-simple design that stands out by being different
Humor or Wordplay Clever headline or visual pun related to your club
Important

Your creative element should enhance your message, not distract from it. Every design choice should serve your communication goal.

Common Design Mistakes

DO

  • Make club name HUGE (readable from 10 feet away)
  • Use high-quality images (no pixelation)
  • Align elements to create clean edges
  • Leave generous margins (at least 0.5" on all sides)
  • Test print in black & white to check contrast
  • Proofread everything multiple times

DON'T

  • Use more than 3 fonts
  • Write long paragraphs (use bullet points)
  • Center-align everything (boring!)
  • Use clipart or cheesy stock photos
  • Make text smaller than 10pt
  • Ignore your brand colors and fonts
